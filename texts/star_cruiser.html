Star Cruiser is a short speculative fiction where we follow Cruiz’s quest to find his missing friend, Zamil, who has disappeared into the gears of the capitalist machine. He finds himself going millions of miles into the stars, looking for him at the heart of a large, opaque company. He must use his body and his brains to track him down, navigating office politics, secret cruising spots, and a crumbling space station.

STAR CRUISER

Cruiz stands up from his chair, throws away his empty coffee cup in the trash, then leaves the cold coffee place. He has been waiting all day for a message, but nothing has come so far. The coffee place is right across the street from his place. It’s early morning, traffic is still light, he crosses the avenue and walks towards his building entrance. He has been waiting for an important mail, waiting to see the delivery car turn at the corner all morning, but nothing so far. The restlessness that took him to the coffee place across the street takes him back to his place.

His front door opens as he approaches, letting him inside his apartment in a swooshing noise. He drops his large coat on the floor and sends his boots flying, leaving mud streaks on the floor. He goes to stand by the large window overlooking the street. There, he stands, head on the glass, lighting up a cigarette. Below him in the street, busy people go on with their lives, fighting the wind pushing in their legs. He stares out blankly.

He inhales deeply on his cigarette, holds the smoke for a second, then slowly releases the smoke towards his bed, lingering above it for a moment, then slowly dissipates. He thinks about his friend Zamil, he’s been waiting for him to come back from his mission, but the silence is deafening. He hasn’t received any news in a while, but he had received a notification from the postal service that an important message was on its way about him and is expected to arrive today. He couldn’t imagine what this was about, and was hoping for good news at least. The nature of their relationship was still unclear to most, even to each other. They are very close friends, keeping their relationship platonic so far, but their strong bond is undeniable. That made his disappearance without a word or a trace all the more brutal and suspicious. He had gone to a remote station to work for this company, Phallacy. a gigantic consortium whose activity goes from simple retail to asteroid mining, as well as large scale armament industry. Zamil hadn’t told him where or what he was going to do for them, his contract was designed behind a well-protected, heavily legislated system that prevented him from talking about it to anyone. He couldn’t think of anything dangerous that he could have been asked to do, Zamil being a kind of administrator rat. He should have come back two weeks ago, but delays aren’t uncommon. Receiving a notification from the postal service however is. His cigarette now a pile of ashes at his feet, he takes another one out, plays with it between his fingers, still staring out the window. He’s lighting his cigarette, staring at the reddening end when in the corner of his eye, he notices the postal truck a block away from his apartment building. He lets the cigarette fall and carelessly crushes it with his foot before rushing at the door. He runs down the stairs, in too much of a hurry to wait for the elevator. When he reaches the mailboxes, the mail has already been distributed. He opens the mailbox to his name, and in it, a white envelope with black edges. He takes it out slowly, his heart beating fast in his chest. He tears it open carefully, taking the light white paper out of it.

The letter also has black edges all around. It is addressed to him, coming from Phallacy. He tries to read it slowly, but his eyes keep jumping to the next line, trying to take in the content as fast as possible. His heart drops when finally, he understands what the letter is about. Like he suspected, something happened to Zamil. Reading through the corporate jargon, he understands that something unexpected happened to him, leading to a sudden and mysterious disappearance that even the authorities there couldn’t figure out.

Given the extreme nature of the mission he was given, they have to legally report it as a fatal disappearance, but that they will keep doing everything in their power to keep looking for him. He reads it again, looking for something written between the lines. The short feeling of relief is hardening in his gut, morphing into silent anger. He takes the stairs back up, slowly, the paper still locked in his grip. His mind is going fast, thinking of what to do next, planning with each step he takes. By the time he reaches his floor he has the outline of a plan in mind to go after Zamil and take the matter into his own hands, knowing full well the extent of the incompetence of the authorities, even more so when a big company is involved. There clearly is something fishy in this, and he intends to uncover it.

He is back in his apartment, straightening the mess he had left behind. He needs a clear space to think about how to get to the station where Zamil was posted. The plan is simple: get to the station, start to investigate, find what happened to his friend then go home, hopefully with him. The hard part is to get there. He could try to take a shuttle and approach them on his own, but this is a high security station, alone, he has no chance. So, he could get there sneakily with the help of a smuggler, but once again, too many blind spots, too many weaknesses in this plan. The more he is thinking about it, the more evident it becomes that he is going to have to get hired by Phallacy at the same station as his friend, and do the investigation from within, as an employee. He sits down heavily on the couch, head thrown back, staring at the smooth ceiling, smells of the city coming through the opened window. Time to set the plan in motion and look up the job offers they have on the station. Phallacy has a total of eleven stations spanning across millions of kilometers in the system, such as research, mining, crops and even headquarters. Because international law does not apply the same way outside the atmosphere, it is very profitable to do tax evasion in outer space. Zamil was working in a scientific one, doing research on a sensitive subject. That leaves the only scientific station they have, Polari Station.

They have a total of seven open positions. The descriptions are vague, the job title cryptic. However, one of them is titled “Custodian”, no description attached to it. Maybe it sounds ludicrous, but that would be an ideal position to fulfill, at the center of everything, invisible and that didn’t require lying too much to get it. But he is going to need to contact some friends to help him create an alter ego, a fake identity to infiltrate without them making a connection between him and Zamil. The plan is set in motion. He decides right then and there to pay a visit to one of his most tech-savvy friends living in town to help him out make a rock-solid fake identity. He packs a light bag with few articles of clothing, a vanity with some essentials, as well as a handful of hardware tools, just in case. He grabs his coat and just like that, he’s on his way.

Cruiz is sitting in a shuttle on its way to the Polari Station, along with five other people with whom he hadn’t introduced himself while waiting on the launchpad. The reactors were making a loud noise until it died out once they left the atmosphere, the shuttle becoming silent, the air no longer rubbing against the fuselage, the burning fuel entirely consumed, the craft now cruising in the void to the secret scientific station.

Getting the job has been simpler than he had imagined. He went to see his friend Mary who helped him getting a new identity off the dark market and together they had made a rock-solid file for his alias. And like a fly to honey, Phallacy had been drawn to him and fell into his perfectly executed trap. He loves that he is using the idea that large companies like these don’t care about their employees to his advantage. Because to them he is nothing more than a number in their file bank, they would never go to extreme lengths to check the legitimacy of a simple custodian, even for a highly secretive research lab. People around him are removing their seatbelts, floating around to look at the view through the portholes, their feet dangling, useless. Most were probably scientists. He remains seated, extending his neck to take a peek outside, seeing a piece of blue in the distance floating in an ocean of black. Everything went very fast from the moment he applied for the job. He hadn’t talked to the company yet, everything happened remotely, automatically, automatized. He only spoke to bots, giving him all the information he needed to be here now. This has been almost too easy so far. He only has a backpack full of clothes; he couldn’t get on board with the tools he had taken from his place, but he did manage to bring in his notebook.

Outside, the planet is getting smaller, invisible after a few hours. Screens play videos on loop where perfect people smile, wearing the company’s logo on their sweatshirt, advertising the good deeds and great culture of Phallacy but always remaining vague enough that it becomes impossible to know what exactly they’re trying to sell you. It could have been dictatorship propaganda for all he knows, selling you a perfect idea, or an idea of perfection.

The journey is smooth sailing, he is getting used to the floating and pushing against every surface to move around the shuttle. Once they start getting close to the station, the crew asks them to buckle back down for safety. Everyone goes back to their seat, and stares at the station coming in closer. It’s a large building-like structure, floating in space. It is more angular than he thought it would be, and a lot of windows cover the outer shell.

It is much bigger that he had imagined, and for the moment, he can only see one face of the building, only glimpsing the extending building behind, sprawling appendages as big as the one in front of them. It starts to make sense how one person could disappear in there and how difficult it can be to try and look for them. As the windows he saw in the distance got closer, the scale reveals itself to him. One window must have been hundreds of meters tall, and as wide. They extend all the way across the façade, tens of them going in every direction. This is the largest building he has ever seen. It takes almost an hour for them to get really close to it from the moment it enters their field of view. A large door opens in the smooth grey surface of the building, swallowing the shuttle in darkness. Lights turn on along a landing dock and the shuttle’s thrusters throw large shadows everywhere. The landing is a bit rocky, everything is vibrating around them, it feels like the craft is going to rip in half. He starts to feel heavy as they get closer to the ground, his body gaining its weight back. It lands on the pad in a thudding noise, a cloud of smoke rising around it.

Now he can feel his full weight in his arms and legs, almost like a relief. It takes a while for everyone getting used to their movements again and then to get down, led by the two pilots going down the ladder. The dock they landed on is an extremely large room, only lit around the landing area, and a narrow path towards an opening in the wall. The ceiling is so high he cannot see where it reaches, the echo dying out in the distance.

They follow the pilots towards the dark opening in the wall. The walls inside are the same grey as the outer walls of the station, Cruiz runs his hand along one to feel it. It is a sort of composite wall, between metal and concrete, cold and solid. They arrive in a small, carpeted room, with empty seats and counters and the pilots tell them to wait here for someone to come to us while they exit through a security door on the left.

A man comes in and welcomes them with a frozen corporate smile, his shiny hair combed back, reflecting the overhead light. Two other men trail behind him, notepads in hand, staring blankly at the group. The small man asks them if the journey has been good for them, all the while the other two men take care of checking the paperwork of each person.

Cruiz’s fake paper gets him through the security without any problems. He is asked to follow one of the men holding a notepad to another part of the complex while the others follow the other man. The young man he is following tells him that he is showing him to his quarters. They walk along grey carpeted corridors, passing empty rooms and closed doors until they reach a large hall resembling the landing site they were at earlier, simply smaller and covered from floor to ceiling with the large windows he could see from outside.

He has a view of a side of the station on a black background speckled with the white stars. Some people are walking along the window, eating and talking. The Polari station is the equivalent of a large city, counting over a million people on board, all working for Phallacy. Not only does it house the equivalent population of a large city, but it also functions as one, a lot of people being on board for the sole purpose of making the station run smoothly. That’s why Cruiz is here. But the details and the expectations of his mission will be revealed to him the next morning.

After walking for a good fifteen minutes, the guide turns towards him, telling him that they’re getting near, they just have to go to the lower levels. He takes him to a spiraling staircase going down. He looks at the bottom of it but it goes so down that it is impossible to see the end of it. The stairs are encased in this grey tube with occasional round windows giving you a view of the whole floor. They go down for what feels like an eternity, along the way, Cruiz asks if there isn’t a more efficient way to get down, a faster mode of transportation. And indeed, there is, but they cost money and at this time of the day, they’re usually taken over by the swarm of people getting on their lunch break. Finally, they reach what they call the Service Floor. Halls and corridors seem smaller here for some reason; There aren’t any windows as well, but a lot more screens showing a wide array of things, mostly ads.

He recognizes himself more in the people that were going here; they’re wearing on their face a fatigue and exhaustion that he can relate to. They end up in the apartment complex part of the floor, after a mazelike journey through corridors. It looks like a hotel corridor there, doors next to each other stretching on an impossibly long gangway. They reach a door with the number 0006 on it. The guide turns to Cruiz, hands him a magnetic card, telling him that everything he will need for his first day at work on the morrow is waiting for him inside. He leaves him standing there, walking towards the other end of the seemingly never-ending hallway, towards darkness.

Finally alone, Cruiz can breathe for a moment; he uses the key to get inside his new living quarters. After a shower, he checks the fridge, a pack of vacuumed-sealed shapeless food is waiting for him. It reads “Microwavable Beef Stew”. He pours the brown mixture in one of the provided bowls and reheats it. As he eats the tasteless food over the kitchen counter, he takes a look at the map of the station. It looks like a gridded city map over five levels, with entire segments greyed out without any information, probably housing labs or other secret facilities. He lets the guide fall once again, leaves the bowl in the sink and crashes in bed. He has to be at work at seven that leaves him about six hours of sleep, even though his body is screaming for more after the fifteen-hour trip he just took. He closes his eyes and within seconds he falls into a dreamless sleep.

An alarm clock wakes him up thirty minutes before his shift is supposed to start. Startled, he jumps out of bed right away. He doesn’t want to be late for his first day, he wants to be as forgettable as possible, the most average man ever. He puts on the uniform they provided for him, a blue overall with a zipper at the front that goes all the way down. On the chest, on the right side, his name is embroidered, on the left side, the logo of the company. He grabs the badge with his card, slides the guidebook and his notebook in his pockets and is on his way. According to the manual, it takes him twenty minutes of walking to get to his workstation. He follows the custom route they have highlighted for him inside. He goes through his “building” area, walking in front of what seems to be hundreds of doors. Some people are going in the opposite direction, not even paying attention to him. Everybody he sees, however, is wearing more or less the same uniform he’s wearing.

He arrives in a large empty hall where in the middle sits a grey cube with a door. He gets close to the door, waves his badge in front of the dedicated reader and the door opens. Inside is what looks like a storage room of cleaning supplies, technical materials as well as construction tools. A woman sits behind a table, paperwork spread out in front of her. He approaches her and introduces himself. She looks up and looks at him as if he were the most insignificant person in the world. She hands him a paper to sign, a liability waiver stating that if anything bad happens, he won’t press charges.

He signs it without looking further, not that it matters. She stands up, and without even looking at him, tells him that his superior will be here shortly and that he can just wait here for the moment. She vanishes, leaving this place as fast as she can, her steps resonating in the distance. He looks around at the room, crammed with crap. He sits behind the table and starts taking notes in his notebook, thinking of ways to start the investigation. Now that he has seen the way the space unfolds within the station, and with the help of the map, he starts to draw out a plan.

He gets interrupted a few minutes later when a tall man with broad shoulders and large arms comes in, with the first genuine smile he has seen since he left the planet. He extends his hand and introduces himself as Bara. His smile is drowning in his dense and dark beard and his eyes are disappearing in bushy eyebrows. Nevertheless, the man can easily be considered good looking. He is Cruiz’s direct supervisor, and he will guide him through his first day, explaining to him the nature of his mission. Bara explains to him that there are hundreds of independent crews like them all over the station, each with a specific area allocated. They never cross paths, they don’t even know who they are, that’s the way it is on Polari Station to avoid any leak of information.

He shows him on the map the area they’ll cover, which is a radius around the current room, as well as some smaller parts on the superior floor as well as the lower one. They have a very polyvalent position, from cleaning to technical upkeeping, even security. Because it is only the two of them, they both have a six-hour shift every six hours; it is an intense rhythm, but it’s not like there is anything else to do on the station anyway.

They have to meet up here every six hours, either to leave or start, until the end of their mission in two months. Inside, Cruiz hopes he’ll be gone by then. Bara being alone for the past few days after the early departure of his previous colleague, the workload has been accumulating so they will have a lot to do today. He starts to write down a list for them to do, writing on a sheet of paper, his large hands grabbing the tiny pen like a toothpick. Routine check of the cameras, a dispute to be resolved without the intervention of police, cleaning due in some alleyways and then maintenance on some equipment. That’s how his first day starts.

He follows the chatty bear to the camera room. He couldn’t have dreamt a better position than to have access to security cameras on the station, that will make his job of finding Zamil hopefully easier. Behind the doors, a small room with nothing but a chair behind a table facing a wall covered with screens. The screens are showing at least twenty different points of view. Bara sits on the chair and starts checking each camera carefully, one after the other.

He explains while checking them that most of them are hidden to most employees. They know that they’re being filmed, they just don’t know from where, although some cameras are open source, meaning everybody can access the feed and see what is happening. The cameras are generally fixed, but some come from the small flying equipment that cruises above the most crowded areas. He shows Cruiz one screen where the camera is moving and shows him how to move it around and zoom. The people on the camera are crystal clear, every detail of their faces, he can even read their lips. Bara keeps on checking that all of the other one’s work and that nothing is obstructing the view or any abnormal activity is happening. He then explains that they have access to every camera on the station – except for the labs – but here, the cameras only cover their perimeter of action, nothing more. The videos are stored for six months and can be checked upon request. Apparently, there is a powerful software behind the cameras that could allow them to use facial and object recognition to spot something specific, but he hasn’t found the way to use it and hasn’t dared to ask around.

Once done, Bara turns around in his chair with a grave air and warns him about a practice that has been happening regarding the cameras. It would appear that a handful of men are using the cameras to cruise each other. It’s only a rumor, nobody has been able to provide concrete proofs but some recordings suggest that through the open-source cameras, and even between the different custodians, some sort of intimate exchanges have been happening.

This is another important and interesting piece of data to know for him, knowing about the parallel ways that exist around the station, where he might be able to find clues as to where somebody can disappear. He will look into it later, keep an eye out, maybe ask around. They leave the camera room expletively, after this, but Cruiz’s heart is full of hope. They leave straight to a commercial area where a dispute between two local merchants has been brewing for the past few days.

Bara says it as if he is some kind of recluse, however everyone they see on their way seems to know him and to nod at him. Bara seems to be a popular guy around here, which is not surprising considering the good-hearted nature he gives off. They arrive in a large hall that looks like a shopping center, with several stores selling a wide range of things. Two sellers are having a dispute about the trash chute that keeps getting clogged and they’re both blaming the other for it and both refusing to pay the maintenance fees for it. So they asked the custodian crew to intervene and try to resolve the problem. The feud is between a small clothing store and hardware store selling spare parts.

Bara asks both the managers to talk outside about the issue and see the heart of the problem but they start screaming at each other, Bara caught in the middle, trying to get them to calm down, but the fight is escalating. The strong presence of the custodian isn’t enough to calm them, quite the contrary, both the managers hide behind the large man while spewing their hate to the other.

Bara looks overwhelmed and his face gives away his annoyance. Cruiz runs to help him out, but he is being told by his superior to not interfere. He thinks for a second, a hand brushing his beard while insults fly above him, then tells the newcomer to continue the list of tasks as best as he can, and that he will come back to him before the end of the shift. And just like that, Cruiz was thrown into his job on the station. He looks at his notes and his map. An area is underlined where cleaning is necessary, and two other spots are circled where maintenance is needed. He walks back to the storage room to get the cleaning equipment.
